# Task 04 – Energy and Momentum in a Collision

## Problem Statement

A $0.5 \text{ kg}$ block slides down a frictionless track from a height of $3.0 \text{ m}$. At the bottom, it collides and sticks to a $1.5 \text{ kg}$ block that is initially at rest.

Determine the speed of the combined mass immediately after the collision.

## Theory

The problem has two stages:

1. Conversion of gravitational potential energy into kinetic energy during the descent.
2. A perfectly inelastic collision at the bottom, where momentum is conserved.

Before the collision, for the moving block,

$$
mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2
$$

During the collision,

$$
m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v_f
$$

Since the second block is initially at rest,

$$
v_2 = 0
$$

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Find the speed of the first block just before collision

Use energy conservation:

$$
mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2
$$

Cancel $m$:

$$
gh = \frac{1}{2}v^2
$$

$$
v = \sqrt{2gh}
$$

Substitute $g = 9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$ and $h = 3.0 \text{ m}$:

$$
v = \sqrt{2 \cdot 9.81 \cdot 3.0}
$$

$$
v = \sqrt{58.86} \approx 7.67 \text{ m/s}
$$

Thus the $0.5 \text{ kg}$ block reaches the bottom with speed

$$
v_1 \approx 7.67 \text{ m/s}
$$

---

### 2. Apply conservation of momentum in the collision

Let:

$$
m_1 = 0.5 \text{ kg}
$$

$$
m_2 = 1.5 \text{ kg}
$$

$$
v_2 = 0
$$

Since the blocks stick together,

$$
m_1 v_1 = (m_1 + m_2)v_f
$$

Substitute values:

$$
0.5 \cdot 7.67 = (0.5 + 1.5)v_f
$$

$$
3.835 = 2.0\,v_f
$$

$$
v_f = 1.9175 \text{ m/s}
$$

## Final Result

The speed of the combined mass immediately after the collision is approximately

$$
v_f \approx 1.92 \text{ m/s}
$$

## Interpretation

The first block gains speed by converting gravitational potential energy into kinetic energy. During the collision, momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is not conserved because the collision is perfectly inelastic.

The final speed is much smaller than the initial speed of the moving block because the total mass after the collision is larger.
